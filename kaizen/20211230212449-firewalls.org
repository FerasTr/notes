* What is a Firewall?
A firewall is a device within a network responsible for determining what traffic is allowed to enter and exit. More specifically, firewalls determine if traffic can travel across open ports.

We can permit or deny a packet from entering or exiting based on numerous factors such as:
- Where the traffic is coming from?
- Where is the traffic going to?
- What port is the traffic for?
- What protocl is the traffic using?

Firewalls perform packet inspection to determine the answers to these questions.

Firewalls can be categorised into 2 to 5 categories. The two primary categories are:
+ Stateful
    This type of firewall usese the entire information fomr a connection; rather than inspecting an individual packet, this firewall determines the behaviour of a device based upon the entire connection.

    This firewall type consumes many resources as the decision making is dynamic.

    For example, a stateful firewall could allow the first parts of a TCP handshake that would later fail. Or if a connection from a host is bad, it will block the entire device.
+ Stateless
    This type of firewall uses a static set of rules to determine whether or not indiviudal packets are acceptable or not.

    This firwall type usese much fewer resources than alternatives, but they are much dumber. They are as effective as the rules that are defined within them, if a rule is not exactly matched, it is effectively useless.

    For example, a stateless firewall will block bad packets from a device but not the entire device. These firewalls are great when receiving large amounts of traffic from a set of hosts (DDOS).
